I was knee deep with the team through the entire thing—from defining problems, to scoping down the work, to executing on visual design, to building dashboards, to working with engineers to polish to the final product. The team included two web engineers, one iOS engineer, a product manager, a writer, and a researcher.

User problems and Paper problems
Paper didn't work the way people worked

While we envisioned Paper as a collaborative tool for work, there were clear limitations to the product:

It was impossible to create in-house templates that mah every conceivable team's processes.

Workarounds to copy and paste were a compromise for users who didn't feel like Paper is a tool that works for the way they work.

Teams struggled to have consistent processes across projects which hindered their abilities to be efficient and effective.

Templates we offered during our onboarding experience—limited and not customizable.
Measuring success
A lever for activation and collaboration

Our goal was to create a differentiated feature that would make Paper appealing, activate people and their teams, and ultimately retain them in the long-term. Metrics that measured how valuable the feature would be were:

Percentage of all docs created from templates

Percentage of people collaborating on docs creating from templates

Scoping a minimal viable product
Workflows that would enable us to test our hypotheses

We needed to strike the right balance between launching quickly while still creating a feature that was valuable and solved our users' core problems.

I explored a few original workflows for user stories based on support feedback and past research studies:

As someone who manages team processes, I want to be able to create canonical versions of docs so that our processes are consistent and repeatable.
As someone on a team, I want to make changes to templates so that our processes can improve and everyone seamlessly adapts new processes.
As a new member of a team, I want to be find the templates I need for my job so that I can get up and running as quickly as possible.

From there, we broke down the components that would be require to fulfill these workflows.

First was the template library—a place for people to find all the templates they and their team had created and to create new templates from.

Template library explorations

We landed on creating a reusable dropdown component that we'd attach to different entry points. While we knew it wouldn't scale, it was fast to build and we also didn't expect people to have that many templates to begin with.

Final template library design

We'd be using the same back-end editor as regular Paper docs but we opted for a darker header bar because we'd been using that pattern on one of our other products, Showcase, and it provided the most contrast to editing a doc.

We started out by using adding the template library to all of the existing surfaces where we'd enable doc creation.

Mental model mismahes and surprising data

Engineers were able to build a quick and scrappy version of this without the bells and whistles of UI polish. We released it internally to let people play with it.

While we originally weren't sure where the best entry points were for the template library, we received a bunch of internal feedback that adding it in the doc header made creation of docs feel slow and disrupted people's workflows.

We were originally skeptical that people would want to create docs from templates directly from folders, but the data we pulled found that doc creation from folders was the third most popular place to create docs from.

We also conducted 10 interviews with people who had use cases for templates, to understand how templates could fit into their workflows, as well as to get usability feedback on our early build. Places where our assumptions broke down were:

  • Templates themselves weren't collaborative—usually one person made them and shared them with their team to use
  • The "plus" icon was confusing
  • People didn't associate "blank doc" as a template
